Combining points and meeting people

Why your layover strategy might be the best dating filter you never asked for.

Points people already think in segments: optimal routes, strategic layovers, lounge access. What if the same logic that gets you to Tokyo on one ticket could also surface who else is in the same airport at the same time—without ever revealing your flight number?

We’re not saying you should book trips for love. We’re saying the way you already travel (combining points, stacking layovers, choosing the 6am because it’s cheaper) creates a hidden graph of “who could I run into.” Most dating apps ignore that. We don’t.

Layovers as a filter

Someone who’s willing to add a four-hour layover in Denver isn’t just optimizing for miles. They’re also signaling something about how they spend time, how they plan, and how much they’re willing to trade comfort for something else. When you match on overlapping time at the same place, you’re matching on a shared constraint. That’s a different signal than a swipe in a void.

Meeting in the middle

Long-distance relationships often start with “we met in the middle”—a city neither of you lives in. Points and award space make that easier: you can both route through the same hub, share a layover, and decide if you want to do it again. Combining points isn’t just about the trip. It’s about who’s on the way.
